“Outpost 31’ is a scenario for Arkham Horror: The Card Game for 2-4
players. This is a print and play scenario that does not use any cards from
the core set.

Scenario Setup

To set up Outpost 31, perform the following steps in order.

1. Choose investigators. Each player chooses a different investigator to
play.

2. Each player assembles his or her investigator deck. The full rules for

deck customization can be found on page 8 of the Rules Reference.

3. Choose difficulty level. There are four levels of difficulty in Arkham
Horror: The Card Game: Easy, Standard, Hard, and Expert. The players
decide which difficulty best suits their group, and they keep this difficulty
for the duration of the scenario.

4, Assemble the chaos bag, Place the chaos tokens indicated below into
the chaos bag, and return the other chaos tokens to the game box.

Easy (1 want to experience the story):
+1, +1, 0,0, 0,—1, -1,-1,-2,-2, @, @, A, ®, ¥ x.

Standard (7 want a challenge):
+1, 0, 0,—1,-1,-1,-2,-2,-3, @, A, ¥, 0%.

Hard (I want a true nightmare):
0, 0,—1,—1, 2, -2,-3, 4,-5, @, @ A, i, %, Box.

Expert (I want Arkham Horror):
~ 0,-1,-1,-2, -2,-3,-3,-4,-5, 8 & A, @, ¥, S.%.

You are now ready to begin at the prologue.

Blood Tests

During the scenario, you will need to perform blood tests on all crew
members to identify potential threats. Once you have spent the required
number of clues, you may draw the top card from the “Test Deck’ and
place it face-down under any chosen crew member without looking at the
result. Each crew member can only be tested once. Various game effects
will prompt you to reveal test results. When this occurs, choose any crew
member with an attached blood test.

When resolving a test, the player should read aloud ifit is ‘Clear’ or
‘Assimilated’. If the test shows ‘Clear’, discard the test result card. That
crew member is safe and you may continue using their static ability as
normal. However, if the test reveals ‘Assimilated’, immediately discard the

test result card, flip the crew member asset to its other side, and resolve the

text on their transformed card.

Ifa Blood Test result is Assimilated, place 1 suspicion on each investigator
at that location.

“Suspicion”

Throughout this scenario, various game effects will instruct you to place
suspicion tokens on Crew Member, Investigators and Ally assets. When
such an effect occurs:

@ Take 1 resource token (from the token poo!) and place it on any Crew
Member asset currently in play as a “suspicion token”.

(Choose wisely! Spreading suspicion evenly may help you avoid imm
ediate consequences ).

@ When a Crew Member, Investigator or Ally asset has 3 or more
suspicion, place 1 suspicion on the scenario card. (Limit once per card per
round.)

The scenario card can hold multiple suspicion tokens, representing the
overall breakdown of trust within the station. When the scenario card
accumulates exactly 3 suspicion tokens: Remove all suspicion tokens from
the scenario card and immediately place 1 doom on the current agenda.
Ifa crew member ( with suspicion tokens on them) has a Blood Test
revealed to be ‘Clear’, remove all Suspicion tokens from them. For the
remainder of the game they have become free from suspicion and cannot
be selected during the ‘place Suspicion tokens step of any card effect’.

Crew members who are ‘Clear’ cannot have clues moved to them from
locations (as a result of a treachery card effects).

“Hunter”
Hunter is a keyword ability.

At the start of the enemy phase, each ready, unengaged enemy with the
hunter keyword moves to a connecting location, along the shortest path
towards the nearest investigator. Enemies at a location with one or more
investigators do not move.

@ Some enemies may target specific prey.

@ If there are multiple equidistant investigators who qualify as “the
nearest investigator,” the hunter enemy moves towards the one who
best meets its designated target. If none do, or if the enemy has no prey
keyword, the lead investigator decides.

@ Ifa hunter enemy would be compelled to move into a location but is
blocked by a card ability, that enemy does not move.

“Massive”
Massive is a keyword ability.

A ready enemy with the massive keyword is considered to be engaged with
each investigator at the same location as it.

@ An exhausted enemy with the massive keyword is not considered to be
engaged with any investigators.

@ An enemy with the massive keyword cannot be placed in an
investigator's threat area.

@ When an enemy with the massive keyword attacks during the enemy
phase, resolve its (full) attack against each investigator it is engaged
with, one investigator at a time. The lead investigator chooses the order
in which these attacks resolve. The massive enemy does not exhaust
until its final attack of the phase resolves.

@ When an enemy with the massive keyword makes an attack of
opportunity, that attack only resolves against the investigator who
provoked the attack.

@ A massive enemy does not move with an engaged investigator who
moves away from the massive enemy’s location.

@ If an investigator fails a combat test against a massive enemy, no
damage is dealt to the engaged investigators.

Peril
Peril is a keyword ability.
While an investigator is resolving an encounter card with the peril
keyword, they cannot confer with the other players. Other players cannot

play cards, trigger abilities, or commit cards to that investigator's skill
test(s) while the peril encounter card is being resolved.

Hidden Infection Varient

This mode introduces a hidden role element to the scenario for 3-4
players. It does not change any encounter cards, act or agenda cards, or
core objectives. Instead, it alters the psychological landscape of the game.
When setting up the scenario, perform the following additional steps:

@ Search the scenario cards for each hidden role card (cards 26-29).
Take 1 Infected card and a number of Human cards equal to the number
of investigators minus one.

@ Shuffle these cards together facedown to form the Hidden Infection
deck.

@ Place the Hidden Infection deck facedown next to the agenda deck.
Do not deal these cards during setup.
When Agenda 2a is revealed, if there are 3 or more investigators:

@ Each investigator secretly draws 1 card from the Hidden Infection deck
and looks at it. Do not reveal these cards to other players.

@ Each investigator keeps their Hidden Role card facedown and secret for
the remainder of the scenario.

One investigator may be infected. You will not know who. From the
moment Agenda 2a is revealed, paranoia becomes personal. The infected
investigator retains full control of their investigator as normal. They are
not required to reveal their role, and no game effect can force them to do
so.

~ The Infected investigator wins the scenario if Resolution 2 is reached.

~ If Resolution 1 is reached, the Infected investigator suffers 1 additional
mental trauma.
« Human investigators win the scenario if Resolution 1 is reached.
Throughout the remainder of the scenario, the infected player must
actively try to derail the investigation. While the player cannot reveal
themselves to be infected, they can subtly push everyone towards a
negative outcome. Various treachery cards ask players to place suspicion,
move clues and choose specific crew members (for things to happen to
them). The infected player simply chooses the worst options available to
them whilst pretending it was reasonable choice.

Design Notes

If you made it this far, hopefully you survived and managed to save

mankind.

John Carpenter’s The Thing presents a unique challenge for adaptation.
How do you recreate the slow burn psychological horror of mounting
paranoia in a cooperative card game? The film’s greatest strength lies not
in spectacle or combat, but in the gradual erosion of trust between
characters who must rely on one another to survive.

The Suspicion mechanic serves as the primary vehicle for translating this
tension into gameplay. Unlike traditional doom or horror systems that
punish players directly, suspicion spreads across both Crew Members and
investigators. Minor doubt makes Crew harder to rely on. Greater distrust
begins to fracture cooperation. Left unchecked, suspicion spills over into
the scenario itself and accelerates the agenda.

Importantly, suspicion is not irreversible. Investigators may steady
themselves, and decisive action such as repairing key systems can relieve
mounting pressure. This balance was intentional and hopefully shows
during the gameplay. In the film, fear spreads quickly, but moments of
resolve and clarity push back against collapse. The design tries to create
that same rhythm of panic followed by fragile control.

The Crew assets were designed to sit in an uncomfortable space between
ally and liability. Players can benefit from their abilities, but mounting
suspicion alters how reliable those abilities become. A Crew Member
cleared by a blood test becomes a source of stability. A Crew Member left
untested becomes a lingering question mark. This uncertainty reflects the
film’s central tragedy. The greatest threat is not always the monster in the
open, but the doubt that grows between people.

The scenario’s pacing mirrors a three-act structure. The opening act
focuses on isolation and sabotage, culminating in the destruction of the
helicopter. The second act shifts toward systemic instability, with repairs,
suspicion thresholds, and escalating threats tightening the noose. The final
act abandons restraint entirely, forcing a confrontation with the Thing
itself. Each agenda advancement tries to strip away a layer of safety and
reinforce the sense that Outpost 31 is no longer safe.

For groups seeking an even sharper edge, the optional Hidden Infection
variant introduces a secret role revealed mid scenario. This addition does
not change the mechanics of the encounter deck. Instead, it re-frames
player incentives. The goal was not to create a player versus player
experience, but to amplify the tension that suspicion already creates. The
variant (hopefully) exists as a psychological accelerant rather than a
structural rewrite. Above all, this scenario was designed to make players
ask the same question the film forces upon its characters: not “Can we
defeat it?” but “Can we trust each other long enough to try?”
