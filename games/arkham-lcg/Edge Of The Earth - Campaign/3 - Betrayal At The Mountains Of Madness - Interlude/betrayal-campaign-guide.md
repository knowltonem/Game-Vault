# Betrayal at the Mountains of Madness

*“It is altogether against my will that I tell my reasons for opposing this contemplated invasion of the antarctic, and I am the more reluctant because my warning may be in vain. Doubt of the real facts, as I must reveal them, is inevitable; yet if I suppressed what will seem extravagant and incredible there would be nothing left.”*  
\-HP Lovecraft, *At the Mountains of Madness*

It is 1935. The world has changed, and so have the investigators of Arkham. The great depression brought massive unemployment, while in Europe fascism is on the rise and the spectre of war once more hangs over the continent. The rate of technological change has never been higher, and new aeroplanes and steamships are making the world smaller. Forbidding locations that seemed out of reach only a decade ago are ripe for exploration – locations like Antarctica, still untamed and mostly unexplored. But something ancient lies beneath the frozen continent…

## Additional Rules & Clarifications

#### Abducted

-   Some effects in this campaign can *abduct* assets and enemies. When an asset or enemy is abducted during a scenario, place it in an out of play pile of abducted cards. Players may freely look at the abducted cards.
-   Only *abductable* cards can be abducted (see that entry).
-   Abducted cards count as “in play” for the unique rule. *(You cannot bring a unique card into play if there is a copy in the abducted pile.)*

#### Abductable

-   Not all cards are *abductable*. Ignore any effect which would abduct a card which is not abductable.
-   Only ***Ally*** assets and ***Humanoid*** weakness enemies are abductable. Non-weakness enemies are not abductable.
-   Only humans are abductable. Cards with the following traits are not abductable (current as of Edge of the Earth):
    -   ***Creature***, ***Summon***, ***Geist***, ***Monster***

#### Abductor (Keyword)

-   Whenever an *abductable* asset or enemy leaves play (such as by being defeated) at a location where any ready card has the “abductor” keyword, that card is *abducted* instead.
-   If an investigator is defeated at a location where any ready card has the “abductor” keyword, all of their abductable assets are abducted.
-   A treachery with the abductor keyword is at the location of the investigator resolving it.

#### Inexorable (Keyword)

-   When an enemy with the “inexorable” keyword would leave play, except by flipping or being replaced, instead remove all damage from it, exhaust it and disengage from it. The next time it would ready, it doesn’t. *(To track this, you could rotate the enemy 180 degrees, then adjust the enemy’s rotation to 90 degrees the next time it attempts to ready.)*

#### Manifold (Keyword)

-   When an enemy with the “manifold” keyword would be evaded, do not exhaust it. Instead, choose an investigator at its location. Until the end of the round, that enemy cannot attack that investigator, and ignores that investigator when hunting.

#### Patrol (Keyword)

-   An enemy with the “patrol” keyword moves like a hunter enemy, but rather than moving towards the closest investigator, it moves towards the location specified in brackets. If more than one location fits the criteria specified, the lead investigator chooses a location from amongst the eligible options.

#### Rival (Keyword)

An enemy with the rival keyword may hunt, engage and attack other enemies as well as the investigators.

-   Any enemies which match the criteria are the rival’s adversaries. The rival is the adversary of each of those enemies.
-   An enemy has aloof while at a location with an unengaged adversary. *(If it spawns there, it spawns unengaged.)*
-   Rivals with hunter treat their adversaries as investigators when choosing which location to move to. *(As always the lead investigator resolves ties between equally close options.)*
-   Enemies will attack their adversaries during the enemy phase. Because rivals are also adversaries, their targets may “fight back”. See “Enemies Attacking Enemies” for more details.
-   Massive enemies always count as unengaged for granting aloof. Massive enemies cannot gain aloof.
-   Enemies at a location with an adversary do not move with hunt or patrol.

#### Enemies Attacking Enemies

-   During the enemy phase, after the enemy attack step (3.3) but before the enemy phase ends step (3.4), perform a new *adversaries attack* step.
-   During the adversaries attack step, each ready enemy at the same location as an adversary performs an attack. *(Enemies that attacked investigators during step 3.3 will not be ready.)*
-   To perform these attacks, choose an adversary at the same location and deal it damage equal to the attacking enemy’s damage value. *(Do not deal horror.)* Exhaust the attacking enemy.
-   Enemies defeated by damage from other enemies are not removed from play until the enemy phase ends, so they may still make their own attacks.
-   When massive enemies attack at any point during the enemy phase, they attack each investigator and each adversary at their location.
-   Any enemy with health per investigator, or which gets additional health per investigator, is a “boss”. Whenever a boss deals damage to another boss, multiply that damage by the number of players in the game after applying other modifiers. *(Do not multiply damage dealt by boss enemies to non-boss enemies or vice versa.)*
-   An enemy defeated by another enemy is still defeated for other effects, e.g. Victory X.

#### Expedition Members

During the campaign, allied and enemy characters may join, die or go missing.

-   Assets, enemies and even investigators can be expedition members, missing or deceased.
    -   Track this either by keeping the cards in three piles to represent their status, or by recording their names in the campaign log.
    -   A unique character can only have a single status at a time.
    -   Only story assets can be expedition members.
-   The status of a character affects its inclusion in decks.
    -   Expedition members may be included in decks. Each investigator may include one such story asset in their deck. It does not count towards deckbuilding limits. Between scenarios, investigators may freely add or remove expedition members as long as no more than one is included in each deck.
    -   Unique assets (even non-story assets) that are missing or deceased cannot be included in decks.
    -   Non-unique assets that are missing or deceased reduce the number of copies of that card that can be included in each investigator’s deck by 1 for each copy that is missing. *(For example, if Beat Cop is missing, each investigator may only include one copy of Beat Cop in their deck. If two or more copies of Beat Cop were missing, no copies of it could be included.)*
    -   Any cards which can no longer be included must be removed between scenarios. If a character’s status changes, cards which are eligible again may be repurchased. *(Level 0 cards may be added for free to fulfil minimum decksize requirements.)*
    -   Weakness cards that are missing or deceased are permanently removed from their bearer’s deck. Keep missing weakness cards with the other missing characters.
-   Investigators who are **killed** or driven **insane** are deceased.
-   When choosing an investigator during the campaign (as a replacement or a joining player), players may not choose a character who is missing or deceased.

## Campaign Setup

1.  Choose whether to play the campaign in story mode or challenge mode (see below).
2.  Choose investigators.
3.  Assemble player decks.
    -   Each investigator is a veteran of previous mythos events, and chooses a ***Difficulty*** asset to add to their deck. Which side of the asset card is used depends on whether you are playing in story mode or challenge mode.
    -   Investigators who are chosen later in the campaign to replace retired or fallen investigators also receive a ***Difficulty*** asset.
4.  Choose difficulty level.
5.  Assemble the campaign chaos bag:
    -   **Easy:** +1, +1, 0, 0, 0, -1, -1, -1, -2, -2, {Skull}, {Skull}, {Cultist}, {Elder Thing}, {Tentacle}, {Elder Sign}
    -   **Standard:** +1, 0, 0, -1, -1, -1, -2, -2, -3, -4, {Skull}, {Skull}, {Cultist}, {Elder Thing}, {Tentacle}, {Elder Sign}
    -   **Hard:** 0, 0, 0, -1, -1, -2, -2, -3, -4, -5, {Skull}, {Skull}, {Cultist}, {Elder Thing}, {Tentacle}, {Elder Sign}
    -   **Expert:** 0, -1, -2, -3, -4, -4, -5, -6, -7, -8 {Skull}, {Skull}, {Cultist}, {Elder Thing}, {Tentacle}, {Elder Sign}

### Story Mode and Challenge Mode

Betrayal at the Mountains of Madness has two “difficulty modes” beyond the regular AHLCG difficulty settings: story mode and challenge mode.

This campaign has been balanced to be quite challenging – think The Forgotten Age or The Circle Undone. It’s also been quite carefully balanced to scale with group size, so where regular scenarios are typically much harder in solo and much easier in large groups, in this campaign you’ll find a narrower gap between the two. You might find solo play easier and group play harder for the difficulty setting you usually play on.

During the campaign you’ll sometimes find yourself in situations that require a lot of planning, or where the best available option is the least bad outcome. Many of the scenarios include tense and potentially stressful tactical situations. You will almost certainly not be able to score all victory points on a blind play. If that sounds good, you should play on **challenge mode** which is the default setting the scenarios have been balanced around.

However, if you’re more interested in playing AHLCG for the story or social experience, especially if you tend to play with fun or suboptimal decks, you should consider **story mode**. In story mode each investigator receives a powerful bonus which makes every scenario easier.

Regardless of difficulty mode, every investigator receives 1 ***Difficulty*** asset of their choice at the start of the campaign. The difference between challenge mode and story mode is the side of the asset you use – the story mode side of the card will grant a much stronger ability.

### Deckbuilding Advice

The campaign is fairly enemy heavy in places, and assumes that somewhere around half of your party’s capability is devoted to enemy management. Some scenarios cannot be accelerated by just discovering clues faster, so if your approach is to minimise enemy management and complete scenarios quickly, this may not work well.

Evasion is very useful because of the way it interacts with the rival keyword, but not mandatory, especially at smaller player counts. In some situations evasion will be more efficient and in others damage will be. A few scenarios will be extremely difficult to complete without being able to do at least some damage.

As of Edge of the Earth, a handful of investigator cards will interact badly with the campaign’s mechanics, so I would recommend against them:

-   Cyclopean Hammer and Gene Beauregard both trivialise combat because of the way they interact with the rival keyword. This could be a fun option as an alternative to story mode.
-   Nkosi Mabati’s ability to force certain symbol tokens is is vastly more powerful in this campaign due to the nature of some of the symbol token effects.
-   Geared Up is not a good pick. You won’t always get a calm setup turn.

-   Many cards in this scenario count or target ***Ally*** assets. ## Interlude I: Rendezvous in Argentina

*“We are heading for Argentina,” Dyer tells you, looking over the handrail at the passing coast. “If I have calculated the dates correctly, there we will meet up with an expedition who are scheduled to collect supplies on their way to the Weddell Sea. I have already made contact and I am sure they will welcome our involvement.”*

*On the way, Dyer gives you what seems like a heavily abbreviated summary of the situation. In 1931 he was part of Miskatonic University’s Pabodie Geological Expedition to the Antarctic, during which he saw things which resulted in a firm belief that no one should ever explore the continent in detail. You think back to the news stories of the time, when the expedition reported with great excitement absolutely incredible fossil finds which would certainly trigger a complete re-evaluation of models of life’s evolutionary history on Earth. On his return to the US however, Dyer publicly denounced the earlier reports, and stated that the finds had in fact been not nearly as interesting as initially thought. You ask Dyer what it is that he saw.*

*“I saw… I cannot bear to speak it aloud, even now. I documented it all soon after returning. I will loan you a copy to read on the journey. I gave it to the university – they dismissed it as the work of a fragile mind, shattered by a traumatic experience. The irony!”*

*In 1933 a second Miskatonic expedition led by Professor Moore set out in pursuit of the initially reported discoveries and in defiance of Dyer’s pleas to the contrary. Neither Moore, nor his co-leader James Starkweather, ever returned from the ice.*

*“I knew Moore, you know,” adds Dyer. “He was a student of mine before the war, and a colleague afterwards. He helped organise my expedition, although he was unable to participate – and I think he never forgave me for refusing to explain what really happened, not to mention making every effort to cancel* his *expedition. And of course I was right, though it gives me little comfort.”*

*That same year, the Barsmeier-Falken expedition out of Germany also visited the Antarctic, and returned after a terrible accident killed nearly four-fifths of the expedition’s personnel, including Falken. Disgraced and ruined, Josef Barsmeier killed himself in 1934.*

*“No one really knows what happened to either expedition. I believe both had access to my report, and while my purpose was to dissuade further investigation, I fear it may well have had the exact opposite effect.*

*“And now there is this new threat, this so-called Star Chamber Foundation. I believe them to be nothing more than another cult, unwitting slaves of our alien enemy that lurks in the south. Either my expedition stirred up this evil, or some further foolishness in ’33 did.*

*“So we must go there ourselves and try to put a stop to this. I know that you and my erstwhile colleagues have some facility with these matters, but if I am right then this may well be a threat on a scale you have not yet experienced. I can only hope we, and our allies in Argentina, are enough to meet it. Now go, read my report, and get some rest.”*

*Dyer’s report begins normally enough, telling a mundane tale of Antarctic exploration, but it quickly takes a turn towards the fantastical. He claims to have discovered an ancient, ruined city on the frozen continent, a city over forty million years old. Supposedly, it was built by extinct alien creatures referred to as ‘elder things’ and their slave species, the shape-changing ‘shoggoths’. The incredible fossils the expedition had earlier reported on publicly are found to be hibernating ‘elder things’, who awaken and kill most of the expedition members. Following them to the city, Dyer and his student Danforth encounter the corpses of four out of the eight revived aliens before being discovered by the still-living shoggoth that killed them. They flee for their lives, but while escaping by plane, Danforth looks back and sees something beyond the city which leaves him insensible.*

*The rest of the trip to Argentina passes in uneventful, brooding calm. When the ship docks at Buenos Aires, you find the city even more bustling than you expected. Two obvious reasons for this are clearly visible from the ship: the first is a zeppelin, looming over the city and casting its immense shadow. The second is the ship docked alongside yours. It is an icebreaker steam ship, seemingly brand new, but what stands out about it are the bright red flags that hang from it – flags that proudly display the icon of Germany’s new ruling party – the Nazis.*

*You confront Dyer immediately.*

*“There are no other expeditions this year, and we do not have the resources to organise one of our own,” he replies. “I am sure that the Germans have some dark purpose in Antarctica – doubtless they seek to exploit the very powers that I warned of in my report. If that is so, then it seems to me it is better to be well-positioned to confront and stop them than to leave them unobstructed.”*

*The Germans clearly recognise Dyer from his expedition, and at his recommendation welcome your party aboard their ship, the* Herman Wirth*. They proudly announce that they are the first large-scale expedition funded by the* Ahnenerbe*, an archaeological society dedicated to the pursuit of evidence of Aryan – and specifically Germanic – superiority. Exactly what they hope to discover in uncharted Antarctica they are less clear about, but you can’t help wondering if their eagerness to welcome Dyer has something to do with it.*

## Scenario II: In the Company of Monsters

*Herr Schmidt, the expedition leader, gives you a tour of the* Herman Wirth *on your first day at sea. It is an impressive ship. The most distinctive feature is the massive mooring mast that rises from the aftercastle for the convenient docking of the zeppelin that looms overhead, keeping pace nearby. Your inquiries about visiting the airship are rebuffed – the attempt would be hazardous while at sea, and only to be even attempted in an unimaginable emergency.*

*Other than the mooring mast, the ship seems to be something of a hybrid of cargo and  passenger ships, with an unusually large number of staterooms and an oversized superstructure. Huge loading cranes are tied down on the deck, ready to unload onto the ice upon arrival. Your group have been assigned shared cabins in the aftercastle with the engineering crew – poor quarters, but you’ve slept in worse places.*

*“The ship was built to our exact specifications,” explains Herr Schmidt proudly as you walk along the deck in the bright sunlight, “We have the latest steam-powered, turbo-electric drive. The* Ahnenerbe *anticipates many such expeditions to the forlorn areas of the globe in search of our ancestry and… perhaps even greater things. But you would know more about that than me, I am sure.”*

*Schmidt does not seem to be a man of science, but rather a political figure within the Nazi party attached to the* Ahnenerbe*. He is approachable, but gives away little or nothing in conversation, instead constantly probing you for information regarding your previous exploits.*

## Scenario III: Cold Heart

*The ship arrives at the ice on the afternoon of December 16th, 1935. *Professor Dyer takes you back to the partially buried cabin where you have quartered. ## Interlude III: The Trial

*“In conclusion, the proposed expedition must not be approved. Thank you.” Dyer resumed his seat. The small panel of academics, mostly from Miskatonic University’s geology department, looked on, appearing unimpressed.*

*“Now come, Professor Dyer,” said Dean Armitage, chair of the proceedings, “Your objection is based on a tale most extraordinary, but the evidence on offer is not commensurate.”*

*Armitage thumped the freshly printed manuscript in front of him, which sat next to a thick and disordered notebook of scribblings and drawings. “We were promised photographs – many photographs, in fact, but at least one would be ideal – and instead we have only your sketches.”*

*“As I told you, the photographs were stolen!” shouted Dyer, “I don’t know if it was poor Danforth, or some third party who realised their value. *“But William,” interjected Professor Moore. Moore was a younger man than Dyer, an ex-student in fact, now a respected geologist in his own right, “We have only your word that they existed at all. I don’t mean to accuse you of lying-“*

*“And yet what else can your insinuation mean!”*

*“-I* don’t *mean to accuse you of lying. Your experience with… with Lake and the others was truly horrifying. *Dyer looked down, “I admit that it must seem fanciful to one who did not witness the events themselves.” He looked up, “But consider the accuracy of the text itself. *“Professor Dyer,” said Armitage, “No one doubts your geological expertise. Why, I expect that even were you in Danforth’s state you could hold your own on the subject of pre-Cambrian strata, but that is not in and of itself enough to make your word beyond question.”*

#### Fire Tokens

Some effects in this scenario can add fire tokens to locations, marked using damage.

Locations with fire tokens lose ***Cold*** and ***Dark***. At the end of the enemy phase, each enemy and investigator takes 1 damage for each fire token on their location.

Investigators may attempt to evade fire tokens at their location as if they were engaged enemies with an evade value of 2. If a fire token is evaded, discard it.

#### Flipping and Replacing

Effects in this scenario may require you to flip or replace cards. Treat these in the same way – when a card is flipped or replaced, what happens depends on whether the type of the card changes.

If the type remains the same (for example an enemy that flips to or is replaced by another enemy):

-   The card does not enter or leave play, it simply remains in play.
-   Any tokens or attachments (such as damage or apparatus) remain with the new card.
-   The state of the card remains unchanged (for example its location, or whether it is exhausted).

If the type changes (for example an enemy that flips to or is replaced by a story asset):

-   The old card counts as leaving play, and the new card counts as entering play.
-   Any tokens or attachments are removed.
-   The state of the card is as if it just entered play, with the exception that it enters play in the location the old card was in (if there was one).

Cards that “cannot leave play” can still be flipped, even though that could cause the card to leave play through the type changing.

## Interlude VII – The Whole Story

#### Part 1 – To the Dark Tower

*As you exit at the base of the citadel, the battle between the alien forces has completely cessated. On a nearby, flat area you find a large plane with the engines idling – Danforth must have used this to reach you. The paint on the plane has been scoured by Antarctic winds, but the signage of the Starkweather-Moore expedition is still visible, along with the plane’s name – the* Weddell*. Your group climbs aboard, and with minimal delay you head up into the kaleidoscopic sky.*

*This is your first time seeing the city from the air, and as it stretches beneath and behind you it looks serene and unchanging, despite the battle that raged across it only recently. Looking east, you see the so-called mountains of madness, whose dark, barren peaks climb as high as 30,000 feet into the air by Dyer’s reckoning.*

*Suddenly another one of the strange quakes or convulsions hits the city. High up in the air, you do not feel the ground move, but you can clearly see the visual distortion. It is like a wave, rolling in from the west and spreading out across the city. Wherever it reaches, everything changes. Where there was ruin and a thick glacial layer, now the city lies intact, spiralling domes rising even higher into the sky and thick jungle winding its way around and between the buildings. As quickly as it came, the vision fades, and you are once more looking out across a vast, frozen ruin.*

*Looking towards the west, from where the strange effect emanated, there is nothing but a prismatic haze and an almost featureless, blasted plateau. A barely visible riverbed leaves the city to the west, and recalling its significance in certain murals, you follow its course. After half an hour or so, you dimly see the tips of mountains appearing beyond the horizon. As they rise into view, it quickly becomes apparent that they dwarf even the mountains behind you. Dyer has his face pressed eagerly against the foggy windscreen. The fierce, craggy mountains behind seem like the mere foothills to these titans, and their shape and position has a curious regularity to it – a sawtoothed line of savage black triangles.*

*As you come even closer, you make out a huge, strange shape directly ahead of you. The mountain range is not an even line – it recedes slightly in a semicircular indentation, and nestled into that recess is a tremendous storm, a thick tornado which rises amongst the mountain peaks before gradually dissipating into the thin air. It is entirely fixed in place, and completely regular and symmetrical in shape, speaking to a profoundly unnatural origin.*

*Near the base of the storm is a tiny, but extremely bright, blueish light. You descend towards it, and can soon make out the shape of a tower, squat and brutal, some 400 feet high, with the light emanating from the very tip. There is nowhere near the tower to land, and the ground as far as you can see is torn into rippled patterns radiating from the storm. The old river bed runs nearby, though, and you head towards it to land a couple of miles away.*

*From the ground, the tower sits within a snow-filled valley, and the huge tornado soars into the sky beyond it, with the formidable mountain range climbing even higher still. Descending into the valley, you cross the long distance to the tower. It is made of the same black stone as the city, but polished and seemingly intact despite the long aeons. Near the base is a huge opening, but to your surprise it is blocked by a clearly recent addition – painted metal fills the gap, and a large but clearly human industrial door blocks your entrance. Even more incongruously, at the center of the door is a large, brass door knocker.*

*Unsure of what else to do, you knock on the door*.

#### Part 2 – A Civilized Welcome

*For a moment, there is no answer. You weren’t sure what you expected when you knocked on the door of this strange tower at the very end of the Earth. You step back to search for another entrance, when suddenly there is a shearing sound of metal scraping against stone, and the door begins to open. You ready yourselves for a fight.*

*The man who opens the door is a tall, serious looking fellow with a neatly trimmed beard and horn-rimmed glasses.*

*Dyer gasps. “Moore! It is you! What is- what are-“*

*“Professor Dyer, thank goodness you’ve come,” replies the man, evidently Professor Moore of the Starkweather-Moore Expedition. “Have you brought the survivors from Boston with you? Good! Excellent! We still have a chance then! Come in, come in, you must be freezing, not to mention short of breath. Is Danforth with you? No? Regrettable.”*

*He steps back, allowing your entire entourage to troop inside. The tower is warm, and the air somehow thick with oxygen. You greedily gulp in lungfuls of the stuff, only now realising how faint you were feeling from the thin air of the plateau. Moore leads you up a long, dark ramp – the weathered, patterned stone reminds you of the tunnels in and around the alien train system – and into a wide, open hall filled with the now-familiar murals. A few items of human furniture – tattered couches, end tables, small bookshelves and the like – are nestled between cyclopean, alien pillars.*

*Some of the academics sink wearily into the available seating, while others excitedly study the room. One mural dominates the wall – a crude, late work that depicts what appears to be nothing less than a ritual human sacrifice. Human – or perhaps humanoid – heads are being offered up by the city builders to some tall, many-headed deity. The depiction is quite unsettling, and very unlike the otherwise irreligious aliens.*

*“I’d offer you all a glass of brandy,” says Moore, “But I’m not sure I have enough for everyone. It costs a tremendous amount to bring it in from Cape Town, and our resources are stretched as it is. Professor Dyer, you’re leading this expedition I assume? If everyone else could just wait here for a moment, I have something I must discuss with him.”*

*Moore takes Dyer gently by the arm and leads him towards one of the gloomy archways. Dyer beckons for you to follow.*

*“What in God’s name is happening here, Moore? You’re alive? What have you been doing?”*

*“Lake woke them up,” replies Moore, “And thank the Lord he did. They arrived just in time. The consequences otherwise would have been dire.”*

*Beyond the doorway, a spiralling ramp leads up to another level. The chamber is five-sided, with more archways leading away. Above you, the ramp climbs higher, and a tangled mess of pale, fleshy roots follows it upwards. As you watch them, the roots slowly pulse and writhe, as if breathing.*

*“Welcome to the Star Chamber,” says Moore, gesturing around. “A little joke on my part, but a fitting one. King Charles’ Star Chamber meted out justice from a secret court, and here we are forced to do the same.”*

*A man in a plain suit emerges from one of the side rooms. Moore nods with him, and the two quickly converse in what you think is Afrikaans. The other departs, and Moore leads you further up the ramp.*

*“We have made useful contacts in the Union of South Africa. The government there is not entirely aware of the nature of my associates, but they are very interested in the technology we have to offer, and we have found them ideologically compatible.”*

*“Your associates… Moore, you didn’t-?”*

*“They’re called the ‘Qhrell’. That is to say, that’s the name given to them in the Pnakotic Manuscripts. Their name for themselves is a sort of whistle I haven’t been able to emulate. They’re everything you thought: intelligent, industrious, civilised, but so much more!”*

*At the top of the ramp, you emerge into a chamber so high that it fades into mist high above you. Further ramps ascend still further, but around and around them grows a thick jungle of vines, interlaced with a crystalline lattice of dizzying complexity.*

*“It’s a computing machine, William! Like a differential analyser, but impossibly complex. It maintains the black pit, you see, to stop- I’m getting ahead of myself. I’m sorry, but we don’t have much time.” He glances quickly at his wristwatch. “They imprisoned it accidentally, you see. Something much too powerful, much too large for the prison. It can’t get out, William. It will destroy the Earth, just annihilate it, in moments. Nothing can stop it. Not my associates, certainly not humanity. Our only hope is to keep it trapped, don’t you see?*

*“All their resources, their whole civilization, they put it to keeping the prisoner trapped. They struggled with it for millions of years, William, their civilization fell, but they kept it trapped. The plateau itself, you see, it was all part of the machine. A jungle grew for hundreds of miles – a biological network, a living nervous system, supplementing the crystal matrix in the tower. Then the cold came, and the jungle froze, and the city was abandoned, but they still kept it trapped.”*

*You reach a wide platform, a sort of landing amongst the jungle, and Moore leads you along a precarious walkway to the west. Shattered crystals crunch underfoot, and it quickly grows colder. Up ahead, you see that the western side of the tower has broken open, and the howling winds of the storm reach inside. The jungle recedes, and huge swathes of the crystal lattice near the breach are simply gone.*

*“The tower was damaged, the machine weakened. Without the jungle, they had to find other materials. They made… sacrifices. Pilgrimages from deep in the warm sea to this tower, to maintain and repair it. Then, some time in the 1830s, an explorer named Arthur Gordon Pym somehow made it here, to the tower. He damaged the vents – the heat, they take the heat away, to keep it trapped, to keep it frozen. He damaged the vents, and the prisoner began to escape, but your – Lake’s – things, they found the tower, they repaired it, just in time. They kept it operational, but it’s been weakened. We have to keep it operational!”*

*Looking out through the gaping hole in the tower, you see the western end of the snowy valley stretching towards the storm. Odd, triangular, dark patches are evenly distributed across the area, and it takes you a moment to realise the snow is melting.*

*“Even now the prison is breaking down. We have been doing all we can, but the materials, the materials are so hard to locate. I advise you not to look into the storm itself.”*

*Instinctively you look up, and just for a moment, a crack forms in the wall of the tornado. Inside, all is black.*

#### Part 3 – What Must Be Done

*Your mind’s eye spins beyond all of time and all of space. You see the primordial ocean, you see the nuclear chaos, you see the mist and the darkness at the start of everything. Then you feel the cold, the stillness, the cessation of all existence, for uncountable cycles, and you feel the unending, unquenchable need to escape, to be free. Then you see the future – the prisoner, freed. All of Earth, all of space, all of our reality, crumbles to dust in an instant under its entropic presence. You know for sure that should it be freed, this will come to pass.*

*But this is not the first threat to all of time and space you have faced. Wrenching yourself free of the vision, you turn on Moore and tell him as such.*

*“You do not understand,” replies Moore sadly. “What you have seen… the beings you have faced in the past. They were shadows. Mere vestiges, avatars, of things that lie beyond the limits of what is possible in our realm. This one, this prisoner, is here. All of it is here, in one place, subject to a set of physics that impose no more limitation upon it than a sieve imposes upon a limerick. You think you can defeat it with shotguns and dynamite? An entire civilization of our betters could do nothing but hold it in place! Come, I have one final thing to show you.”*

*He turns and heads back east along the walkway. The air grows warmer as you move back into the jungle. You cross the open platform and continue east, the walkway winding to and fro amongst the thicker trunks.*

*“I never tried to contact you, William. I wasn’t sure how you’d react. I know you were opposed to our expedition. Now you’re here, though, now you’ve seen what is at stake – I need your help. I need all of your help.”*

*At the end of the walkway, a semicircular platform fills the eastern side of the chamber. The gnarled and twisted coils of the thick jungle vines twist across it before rising and disappearing into the mist above you. In the middle of the platform, entwined with and framed by the pulsating vegetation, is a grotesque sight. A corpulent pillar of flesh, covered in dozens – hundreds – of the same, awful thing.*

*It is a column of human heads.*

*The heads vary in age quite substantially. Most are just skulls, some with scraps of flesh still clinging to them. Some are much more recent though, skin and hair still intact, and you notice then that they are not just intact, but alive. Unfocused eyes twitch, and mouths murmur incoherently. Thick rolls of some slime ooze down from above, coating the whole affair in a shiny, protective layer.*

*One of the alien creatures occupies the platform, and from its height you recognise it as one of the atavistic survivors – a being forty million years out of time. It lifts a large, round bowl reverently up towards the pillar. You cannot see the contents from below, but suddenly part of the pillar detaches and extends downwards. The shoggoth reaches into the bowl, plucks the fresh head, nervous filaments trailing from it like the fronds of some sea ray, and carefully fixes it in place, weaving the nerves into the pillar. As the shoggoth withdraws, the new head’s eyes flick open, and it adds its voice to the pillar’s chorus.*

*A sound draws your attention to Dyer, who is heaving his penguin supper over the edge of the platform. “What have you done?” he asks, wiping his mouth on his sleeve.*

*“What we had to, William,” replies Moore. “I was as horrified as you were, at first. They took James- they took my friend to the pillar. We cut the head free, damaged the machine, and almost freed the prisoner. Others had to die to atone for that mistake. Eventually we learned how to communicate with the Qhrell and work together.*

*“They can’t fully repair it. They don’t have the tools, or the industrial base. They found their surviving descendants, deep under the sea, and they’ve been trying to teach them, trying to regain what was lost. They can’t grow new crystal, and they can’t grow new jungle in this climate. They need biological material – brains, William, nervous systems – and as sophisticated as possible, to keep the prison intact.”*

*A shudder runs through the tower then. You recognise it as one of the strange earthquakes, but it is much stronger here. You stumble to your feet and almost slip from the walkway. Moore, clinging to a vine, checks his watch again.*

*“They’re coming faster now. Please, the prisoner is breaking free. When you interrupted the shipment in Boston, we were left with insufficient material for repairs. The crash you caused in the vent system has made travel difficult.*

*“Your friends downstairs, we need their brains. Please, you must help us!”*

-   Proceed to **Scenario VII: Judgement at the Dark Tower**.

## Scenario VII: Judgement at the Dark Tower

*Moore’s question hangs in the air. Is this really the only way? Even if it is, can you go through with what needs to be done?*

*Before you can answer, the sound of Dyer chambering his rifle draws your attention.*

*“You’re insane, Moore! We didn’t come all this way just to murder our colleagues!”*

*The alien on the platform launches itself up into the air and Moore dives awkwardly for cover as Dyer opens fire.*

[Suggested Music](https://itswritingitself.wordpress.com/2020/01/28/ahlcg-arkham-horror-the-card-game-the-soundtrack/#betrayal)

#### Setup Instructions (Paper Version)

-   Gather all the cards from the following encounter sets: *Judgement at the Dark Tower, Miskatonic Offerings, Alien Activity, Alien Abduction, Loathsome Abominations, Agents of the Ahnenerbe, Agents of Cthulhu.*
-   Put the Howling Breach, Synaptic Jungle, The Wall of Skulls, Pool, The Star Chamber, Dissectory, Storage, Heritage Hall, Ossuary, Nursery, Geothermal Well and Flesh Mine locations into play.
    -   Each investigator begins play at The Wall of Skulls.
-   Spawn the William Dyer, William Moore and Caravaggio enemies at The Wall of Skulls. *(Caravaggio enters play aloof due to its rival keyword.)*
-   Set the following cards aside, out of play: Sealed Aperture, The Dream-Eater, The Dunwich Legacy, the *Agents of the Ahnenerbe* and *Agents of Cthulhu* encounter sets.
-   Temporarily set the following cards aside, out of play: Bosch, Durer, Botticelli, the *Agents of the Star Chamber* and *Miskatonic Offerings* encounter sets (they will be used during additional setup).
-   Shuffle the remainder of the encounter cards to build the encounter deck.
-   Skip to **Additional Setup**.

#### Setup Instructions (TTS Version)

-   Click place on the scenario container.
-   Each investigator begins play at The Wall of Skulls.
-   *(Caravaggio enters play aloof due to its rival keyword.)*
-   Proceed to **Additional Setup**.

#### Additional Setup (Paper & Tabletop Simulator Versions)

-   Find the *Agents of the Star Chamber* encounter set. For each player in the game fewer than 4, remove 1 copy of Star Chamber Constable from the game. (For example, if there is only 1 player in the game, remove 3 copies of Star Chamber Constable.) Add the cards you did not remove to the encounter deck.
-   Find the *Judgement at the Dark Tower* enemy cards for Bosch, Durer and Botticelli. For each enemy, if it is deceased, remove it from the game. Otherwise, add it to the encounter deck.
-   Shuffle the encounter deck.
-   Attach cards to locations as follows:
    -   Find the asset or weakness card corresponding to each character who is missing.
    -   Shuffle the missing cards together.
    -   Attach 1 random card from this deck to the Dissectory location, facedown.
    -   Attach the remaining cards from this deck to the Wall of Skulls location, facedown.
-   Sort the Miskatonic Offerings as follows:
    
    -   Find the 28-card *Miskatonic Offerings* encounter set.
    
    -   Find each *Miskatonic Offerings* card whose name matches an expedition member.
    -   Remove each other *Miskatonic Offerings* card from the game.
    
    -   Any player with an expedition member story asset card in their deck permanently removes it from their deck. Find each *Miskatonic Offerings* card that shares a name with any of the cards you just removed. Spawn each according to its spawn keyword. Players cannot include expedition members in their deck for the rest of the campaign.
    -   Shuffle the remaining *Miskatonic Offerings* expedition members to form the *academics deck*.

#### DO NOT READ until the end of the scenario

**If no resolution was reached (each investigator resigned or was defeated):**

-   Proceed to **Resolution 1**.

**Resolution 1**

*It is time.*

-   For each unique ***Elder Thing*** enemy in the victory display, remove an {Elder Thing} chaos token from the chaos bag for the remainder of the campaign. Those enemies are deceased.
-   Each investigator earns experience equal to the Victory X value of each card in the victory display.
-   Take any remaining facedown cards attached to the Dissectory and attach them facedown to the Wall of Skulls instead.
-   In your campaign log, record *X components were added to the machine* where X is the number of facedown cards attached to the Wall of Skulls.
-   If William Dyer and/or William Moore are attached to the Wall of Skulls, they are deceased.
-   Weakness enemies and unique non-story assets attached to the Wall of Skulls are deceased. *(Permanently remove them from their bearer’s decks.)*
-   Story assets attached to the Wall of Skulls are deceased, but this won’t matter for the rest of the campaign if you don’t want to track it.
-   The surviving academics are in no shape to help. Players cannot include expedition members in their decks for the rest of the campaign.
-   Proceed to **Scenario VIII: The End of the Earth.**

*I want these scenarios to be as good as they can be, so any feedback you have would be gratefully received. [Click here](https://itswritingitself.wordpress.com/2019/09/06/betrayal-at-the-mountains-of-madness/#feedback) for feedback and contact information.*

## Scenario VIII: The End of the Earth

*Exiting the tower, you carefully make your way down into the valley beyond. The storm ahead of you stirs furiously, but that is not what attracts your attention. Moore was right – whatever arcane apparatus funnels heat away from this area is clearly malfunctioning or perhaps overloaded. When you arrived at the tower, it sat in a largely seamless basin of snow, but now that snow is starting to melt, revealing what lies beneath.*

*Dark, twisted, grasping remains are gradually pushing through the receding frost. At first you take them for bones – the mass grave of some long-extinct megafauna. As you get closer, you see more clearly that it is flora – the desiccated remains of some ancient jungle, preserved beneath the snow. The visible vines and trunks strongly resemble the living jungle inside the tower, and you realise that this is the external part of that synaptic system. You envisage a great brain, overflowing its skull, poured out across the landscape, frozen and amputated.*

*Around the edges of the valley are partially visible ruins. They appear to be other elements of the same giant system that maintains the storm. Entryways you recognise as the work of the city builders dot the hillside, and from the absence of ice and snow around some of them, you can see which are emanating heat. Dark spots like ants flow from the ruined apertures and down into the jungle. It takes you a moment to recognise the jerky, distorted motion of the stygian cysts. They are flooding down into the valley along clear channels through the snow.*

*The imprisoned god is making its final bid for freedom. You have to stop it before it is too late.*

[Suggested Music](https://itswritingitself.wordpress.com/2020/01/28/ahlcg-arkham-horror-the-card-game-the-soundtrack/#betrayal)

#### Setup Instructions (Paper Version)

-   Gather all the cards from the following encounter sets: *The End of the Earth, Antarctic Cold, Frostbite, Loathsome Abominations, Ancient Evils.*
-   Find the four ***Resolved*** locations and shuffle them together to form the random location deck.
-   Put the four ***Unresolved*** locations (The Elder Pharos, North Valley Side, Thawing Jungle, South Valley Side), The Carven Rim and The Black Pit into play.
    -   Each investigator begins play at the The Elder Pharos.
-   Spawn the Stygian Convergence enemy at the Thawing Jungle.
-   Attach the Nameless Cylinder treachery to the Black Pit.
-   Place the Stygian Swarm enemy card near the agenda deck for reference. This enemy card does not enter play, but the effect on the agenda will create copies of it.
-   Add {Cultist} tokens to the chaos bag until it has a total of 4 {Cultist} tokens.
-   Set Agenda 7 aside, out of play. The agenda deck consists of agendas 1-6.
-   Set the following cards aside, out of play: William Dyer *(Of Unsound Mind)*, Emissary from Yuggoth, Ahtu ibn Sotut, the *Antarctic Cold* and *Frostbite* encounter sets.
-   Shuffle the remaining encounter cards to form the encounter deck.
-   Skip to **Additional Setup**.

#### Setup Instructions (TTS Version)

-   Click the place button on the scenario container.
-   Each investigator begins play at the The Elder Pharos.
-   Add {Cultist} tokens to the chaos bag until it has a total of 4 {Cultist} tokens.
-   Shuffle the encounter deck.
-   Proceed to **Additional Setup**.

#### Additional Setup (Paper & Tabletop Simulator Versions)

-   Check the campaign log:
    -   If William Dyer is Deceased:  
        *You managed to recover Dyer’s satchel and the Orne Library’s copy of the Necronomicon from within it. He has covered the priceless relic with scrawled annotations and scraps of tattered paper. As far as you can determine, Dyer was attempting to recreate the spell to summon Yog-Sothoth.*  
        Choose an investigator to take control of the Necronomicon *(Olaus Wormius Translation)* asset. (This asset is on the reverse of the William Dyer *(Of Unsound Mind)* asset.)
    -   Otherwise:  
        *“My plan! I was going to summon something into their city, something vast and powerful, enough to destroy them and their whole damned civilisation! But here is a far better use – what could possibly oppose a heathen god but another heathen god?”*  
        Choose an investigator to take control of the William Dyer *(Of Unsound Mind)* asset.

-   Check the campaign log. If William Moore is *not* Deceased:  
    *Moore is too badly wounded to directly assist you, but he tells you what he can about the arcane machinery in the valley.*  
    Each investigator starts the game with 1 clue.

-   Check the campaign log. If *you parleyed with the Mi-go*:  
    *The alien representative warily keeps its distance from the dark tower.*  
    Spawn the set-aside Emissary from Yuggoth story asset at The Elder Pharos.

#### Random Locations

-   Many effects during this scenario require you to select a random location. To do so, shuffle the four cards that form the random location deck and use the location that matches the name of the topmost card. (Only the four locations in the random location deck can be selected.)

#### Tracking Trauma

-   Many effects during this scenario will inflict trauma on the investigators. You may wish to keep track of this using damage and horror tokens. Remember that trauma inflicted during play does not automatically deal the same amount of damage and horror. If, during play, an investigator’s physical trauma reaches their health, or mental trauma reaches their sanity, that investigator is **killed** or **driven insane** as usual.

#### DO NOT READ until the end of the scenario

**If no resolution was reached (each investigator resigned or was defeated):**

-   If Yog-Sothoth is in play, skip to **Resolution 2**.
-   Otherwise, proceed to **Resolution 1**.

**Resolution 1**

*Your body is broken. Your mind is shattered. You watch, helpless, as the Nameless Darkness swells larger and larger, its encephalitic gloom filling the sky. Mercifully, you never have to see what becomes of the Earth and our universe.*

-   In your campaign log, record that *the Nameless Darkness dismantled reality.*
-   The investigators lose the campaign.
-   Each investigator is **killed**.
-   Do not read the Epilogue.

**Resolution 2**

*Your body is broken. Your mind is shattered. You watch, helpless, as the Nameless Darkness swells larger and larger, its encephalitic gloom filling the sky. Then a bulbous mass rises before it, and the smaller god’s voice echoes through your synapses.*

*BROTHER, speaks Yog-Sothoth, RETURN WITH US TO THE BLACK THRONE. THE PIPERS WILL PLAY, AND YOU CAN DANCE ONCE AGAIN.*

*An aeon passes in a moment, before both entities begin to rise into the sky. Your vision darkens. The last thing you see is the two gods fading out of our reality, and your last thoughts are the words of the Harmonic Spheres:*

*YES. WE MISSED YOU TOO, BROTHER.*

-   In your campaign log, record that *Yog-Sothoth returned the Nameless Darkness to the Black Throne.*
-   The investigators win the campaign… but never return from Antarctica.
-   Each investigator is **killed**.
-   Do not read the Epilogue.

**Resolution 3**

*Just for a moment, you stare up at the blue sky.*

-   In your campaign log, record that *the Nameless Darkness was sealed in its prison.*
-   If Yog-Sothoth is in play, proceed to **Resolution 4**.
-   Otherwise, check the campaign log. If *you made a deal with the Mi-go*, (this is not the same as *parleyed with the Mi-go*) skip to **Resolution 5**.
-   Otherwise, skip to **Resolution 6**.

**Resolution 4**

*A blazing light fills the valley as Yog-Sothoth floats above it, the god’s great central eye swelling larger and larger. You feel its words blast through your mind.*

*YOU WERE WISE TO CALL UPON ME, it says, FOR THIS IS A WORLD IN DESPERATE NEED OF SALVATION. I SHALL PROVIDE IT.*

-   In your campaign log, record *…but Yog-Sothoth reigned over the Earth for all eternity.*
-   Each investigator is driven **insane**.
-   Yog-Sothoth wins the campaign!
-   Skip to the Epilogue.

**Resolution 5**

*Buzzing fills the air as the alien crustaceans land all around you. It is time to pay the price for their assistance. You wonder what the universe will look like from the inside of a canister.*

-   In your campaign log, record *…but the investigators were taken by the Mi-go*.
-   Each investigator is **killed** – in a manner of speaking.
-   The investigators win the campaign, but their brains never return to the Earth.
-   Skip to the Epilogue.

**Resolution 6**

*You force yourself to your feet and escape back to the tower as ice starts to fill the valley. The remaining Star Chamber agents are fearful, but look to you for guidance. Now the maintenance of the god trap is your responsibility.*

*The continued operation of the god trap mechanism is an all-consuming endeavour. Just growing and expanding the synaptic jungle consumes enormous resources, and as the years pass you are forced to build a complex network of agents and industries just to fund it.*

-   Check the campaign log for how many *seals were placed on the prison.*
    -   4 seals: Record *…and only occasional sacrifices were necessary to maintain that prison.*
    -   3 seals: Record *…and a handful of sacrifices every year were necessary to maintain that prison.*
    -   2 seals: Record *…and a dozen sacrifices every year were necessary to maintain that prison.*
    -   1 seals: Record *…and dozens of sacrifices every year were necessary to maintain that prison.*
    -   0 seals: Record *…and hundreds of sacrifices every year were necessary to maintain that prison.*
-   Each investigator earns 10 bonus experience for facing the Nameless Darkness and coming out alive.
-   The investigators win the campaign!
-   Proceed to the Epilogue.

## Epilogue

-   If *Yog-Sothoth reigned over the Earth for all eternity*, proceed to **Epilogue 1**.
-   Otherwise, if each investigator was **killed** or driven **insane**, skip to **Epilogue 2**.
-   Otherwise, skip to **Epilogue 3**.

**Epilogue 1**

*5430 in the Year of Our Illuminated Master*

*Father Nazgroth,*

*Proles attacked the sector six perimeter again today. Archon Ghatanothoa easily repelled the attack, but still it troubles me. Can they not see? How do they not live in our harmony? The light of the All-in-One fills the skies but somehow they are blind. I do not understand.*

*Our supplies of vitreous humour are running low. Please dispatch more thralls to the western recylotorium. The rector has requested that four parts in ten be from the lucid, although I have doubts that such a demand can be fulfilled.*

*In nomine patris tenebris,*

*Deacon Cyunet*

**Epilogue 2**

*“An extraordinary tale, Professor Dyer.”*

*Miskatonic University President Erwin thumbed again through the typed report, “No other survivors. I am sure the experience must have been quite harrowing for you. I am beginning to think that these Antarctic expeditions are simply too dangerous to pursue.”*

*Ahtu smiled with Dyer’s lips, “As I have said before, sir, I think that might be for the best.”*

**Epilogue 3**

*McMurdo Station, Antarctica, 1998*

*Special Agent Baker Steele slams the metal door behind him, shutting out the howling Antarctic wind. He takes a moment to warm his hands, his breath still clouding in the freezing air, before switching on his flashlight. The iridescent beam reveals very little of the silent, cavernous hangar. The wing of a huge cargo plane looms over him.*

*It’s been a long journey to get here. His missing sister. The traitors within his own department. His abducted partner. Evidence of a shadowy conspiracy dating back to 1935 or beyond. Finally, he has reached the heart of this organisation. Finally, he will learn the truth.*

*Up ahead of him, the silence is broken by a familiar click and a tiny burst of light. The flare of the lighter illuminates the face of the one who killed his father.*

*“You’ve done well to make it this far, Agent Steele,” you say.*

*“You!” shouts Steele, raising his gun, “I know this is where you’ve been bringing the missing test subjects. You’ve been here since the beginning! What are you doing with them? What happened in 1935? Answer me!”*

*You take a long drag of the cigarette, “They went to the wall. They went to the wall and gave their lives so that the human race might go on existing for another worthless day.”*

*“Do you mean they were executed? What are you talking about?”*

*“Come with me, Agent Steele. I will let you gaze into the Nameless Cylinder. You’ve earned that much. A single glimpse will answer all of your questions. If you survive, I hope you will choose to help us. If not… well, the machine always needs more components.”*
